#We have several weeks of data in our dataframe. 
#Let's ensure that we have: 
#                   -7 days between each date recorded
#                   -All stores share the same dates

#First we will order the dates chronologically as data is currently out of chronological order.
df.sort_values(by=["Date"], inplace=True, ascending=True)

#Now let us reset the df's indices for effective row iteration and check for the correct time intervals.
df = df.reset_index()
#Removing extraneous index column.
df = df.drop(columns=['index'])

for i in range(df.index.max()):
    if (df.loc[i+1, "Date"]-df.loc[i, "Date"]).days == 7:
        continue
    elif (df.loc[i+1, "Date"]-df.loc[i, "Date"]).days == 0:
        continue
    else:
        print(f"ERROR: The dates at indices {i} and {i+1} are not 7 days apart!\n     {df.iloc[i,1]} and {df.iloc[i+1, 1]}.\n")
print("Dates are consistently populated.")

Looking at our correlation matrix, there are a few conclusions we can make.

Across 995 days (timedelta accounts for n-1 days) and 45 different stores, we can observe that:

  • There are not any strong correlations between our given factors and weekly sales. Factors such as CPI and unemployment did not show any strong connection to weekly sales. This may be due to the fact that Walmart products are "needs" essential for human survival (food, drink, clothing, etc.)
  • The strongest observed correlation is between average fuel price vs. date. This highlights the steep rise in gas prices seen in 2011 and 2012.
  • Even with the significant increase in fuel prices, we can see that weekly sales remained somewhat consistent.
  • We can conclude that Walmart sales are fairly resilient even in a volatile economy.
